Early Life

Born in the village of Aiho, West Bengal, Momtazuddin Ahmed's journey began on a winter Sunday in 1933. Though official records cite 1935, his father's records remember the cold afternoon of December 17th.

His name, originally Tamizuddin Miah, was changed by a village scholar to Momtazuddin—a name that would eventually echo through the halls of Bengali academia and theater. His education started at the age of four, sparked by the poems taught to him by his elder sister, Anju Bubu.

The Formative Years

At Malda District School, he ranked first in his admission test, beginning a chapter of strict discipline under Headmaster Dr. Enamul Haque. It was here, among the news updates written on the school veranda, that his intellectual curiosity flourished.

Partition and Resilience

The partition of 1947 brought upheaval. Displaced to Bholahat in Pakistan (now Bangladesh), he completed his matriculation at Rameshwar Secondary School. Despite the chaos of the era, his dedication to the Bengali language never wavered.

The Final Act

"Abba, I'm here," the son whispered.

Momtazuddin lay motionless, a muted echo of his vibrant self. His eyes, though open, held no light; his voice, a dry rustle. He managed a final breath at 3:48 PM, leaving behind a legacy that would never be silenced.

Somewhere beyond the sterile white of the ICU, the legendary dramatist was already composing a new drama, his words unfurling like dawn over an unseen stage.

Bon Voyage, Abba.
